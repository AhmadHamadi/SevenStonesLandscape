The Burlington Permit Stack: What Actually Applies

Burlington is stricter about yard work than most of its neighbours, and the reason is one by-law that gets ignored constantly. If you have read elsewhere that a ground-level patio never needs a permit, that advice is not written for Burlington.

Site Alteration By-law 064-2014: the one people miss

Burlington regulates grading on its own track, separate from the Building Code. By-law 064-2014 covers the placing, dumping, cutting or removal of fill and altering the grade and drainage of land by any other means. The City's own list of projects that commonly need a Site Alteration Permit reads like a landscaping invoice: pool installation, yard landscaping or infilling, sidewalk and patio construction, and retaining wall construction. You screen the project against Burlington's Site Alteration Screening Checklist, and a single "yes" means you apply. Fees scale with the size and location of the work area, plus HST, and an amendment can cost up to half the original application fee, which is a good argument for getting the grading right on the first submission.

The by-law exists because altering your grade sends water somewhere, and "somewhere" is usually the neighbour's foundation. That is also the single most common way a Burlington job gets stopped mid-build. We screen the scope before the quote goes out, not after the excavator is booked.

Retaining walls: two thresholds, not one

Everyone knows the 1 metre rule: over 1 metre, Burlington's grading and drainage plan requirements call for a Professional Engineer's design, and the building permit follows. The threshold almost nobody quotes is 0.6 metres, above which a wall may require a barrier. That one bites on seat-height walls at the top of a grade change, where a two-course block wall suddenly needs a guard designed into it. Two further triggers that generic advice omits entirely: a wall carrying a surcharge — a driveway, a parked vehicle, a patio bearing on the backfill — changes the load case and needs engineering at effectively any height, and a wall inside a Conservation Halton regulated area or an NEC development control area needs those approvals no matter how short it is.

Private Tree By-law 040-2022: where it stops

Burlington's private tree rules apply only within the Urban Planning Area Boundary. Inside it, By-law 040-2022 requires a permit to injure or destroy a tree 20 cm in diameter (8 inches) or greater, measured at 1.37 m from the ground. Heritage trees and endangered, threatened or at-risk species need additional approvals on top. Rural North Aldershot lots outside that boundary are not caught by 040-2022, a real distinction, and one that a contractor who only works the suburbs will get wrong in both directions. Outside the boundary, escarpment lots usually pick up NEC requirements instead, so "no tree by-law" rarely means "no approval."

Heritage: the Burlington Avenue-Ontario Street HCD is new

By-law 08-2026 designated the Burlington Avenue-Ontario Street Heritage Conservation District, passed March 2, 2026, with the Notice of Passing issued April 9, 2026 and a 30-day appeal window to the Ontario Land Tribunal. Inside an HCD, the district plan governs alterations and streetscape-facing hardscape needs municipal heritage approval: front walkways, driveway aprons, front-yard walls and steps all sit in scope. The designation is recent enough that quotes written for those streets frequently ignore it. We confirm district status and check the Municipal Cultural Heritage Register with Burlington Community Planning during the site visit.

Pool enclosures are municipal, not regional

City of Burlington By-law 074-2005 sets the enclosure at not less than 1.2 m and not more than 2.0 m from the ground, with self-closing gates and a self-latching device on the top interior side. Note the maximum: a tall privacy fence is not automatically a compliant enclosure. We set the enclosure line before the surround is designed, because moving a fence after the coping is laid is expensive.

Burlington Soil, Geology and Drainage Reality

Burlington's ground changes as you move north from the lake, and the base build has to change with it. The dominant surface deposit across the Hamilton–Halton area is Halton Till, a clayey silt to silt till laid down by the last major glacial advance, which reaches thicknesses around 20 metres. That is the material under most of Burlington's suburban middle, and it is the reason drainage — not paver choice — decides whether a job survives 15 winters here.

Clayey silt till behaves in a specific and annoying way. It holds water, it heaves when that water freezes, and it does not let a French drain "find" an outlet on its own. Ontario frost drives roughly 1.2 m down in this area, which is well below any patio base, so the goal is never to out-dig frost. It is to keep water out of the base so there is nothing to freeze. On Halton Till that means a non-woven geotextile separation layer is not optional: without it, clay fines pump up into the open-graded stone under traffic and the base turns into a sponge within a few seasons.

Moving north and west into Aldershot and Tyandaga, the till thins over Niagara Escarpment bedrock and topsoil gets shallow and rocky. Excavation stops being a machine problem and becomes a drilling problem; wall anchoring and base depth both get reconsidered. Down at the lake in Roseland and Shoreacres, soils are typically sandier and drain better, which sounds like good news, and mostly is, but better drainage plus an eroding bank is exactly the combination Conservation Halton's shoreline hazard mapping is written for.

The newest subdivisions are their own category. Alton Village and Orchard were built by stripping topsoil, running loaded machinery over the clay subgrade for months, and then dressing it with a thin topsoil layer. The subgrade under that lawn is compacted well past anything a root or a downspout can cope with. It is why those neighbourhoods generate so much of our grading and drainage work within a decade of occupancy, and why the fix is usually subgrade correction rather than another surface swale. See backyard drainage and water pooling in a yard for how we approach it.

One Burlington-specific access note: the lakeshore's older estate streets and the downtown core have narrow driveways, mature canopy and, in the HCD, protected streetscape. A 400 sq ft rear patio that a machine could build in three days in Alton Village becomes a wheelbarrow job in Roseland. That is a real line item, not a markup.

Conservation Halton Permit Considerations in Burlington

Conservation Halton is the right authority for every watershed in Burlington, and it now regulates under Ontario Regulation 41/24, which replaced the older O. Reg. 162/06 that a lot of contractor websites still quote. Under 41/24, CH regulates watercourses, valleylands, wetlands, the Lake Ontario shoreline, hazardous lands, unstable soil and bedrock, and lands adjacent to those features. A permit is required before any development inside the regulated area.

The part worth understanding is that the lakefront limit is not a flat number. The shoreline hazard is measured to the furthest landward extent of the combined flooding, erosion and dynamic beach hazards, plus an allowance of up to 15 metres beyond that aggregate. Two neighbouring Lakeshore Road properties can therefore have meaningfully different regulated limits depending on bank height and erosion history. Anyone quoting you a single generic setback for the whole Burlington waterfront has not looked at your frontage. We pull the CH mapping for the specific address at quoting, not after the material is on site.

In practice this affects Roseland and Shoreacres lakefront terraces, shoreline steps and armour stone; properties near Burlington's urban creeks and their valleylands; and Aldershot and Tyandaga lots sitting on hazardous or unstable slope. Conservation Halton focuses on three main watersheds, Grindstone, Bronte and Sixteen Mile, plus 18 smaller urban creeks across Burlington and Oakville. Grindstone Creek is the Burlington one most homeowners underestimate: it drains roughly 100 square kilometres through rural Hamilton and Burlington and then straight through Waterdown, Aldershot and Bayview before discharging to Hamilton Harbour, supplying about 14 percent of the water flowing into the harbour. On the east side, Tuck, Appleby, Shoreacres and Sheldon creeks run through built-out neighbourhoods where a back-lot valley edge is easy to miss until the permit is refused. Where a project is regulated, we coordinate the CH submission, supply engineered drawings, and sequence it alongside the Burlington building permit and Site Alteration Permit so the approvals run in parallel rather than end to end.
