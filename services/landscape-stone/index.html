Is landscape stone better than mulch in Ontario?

Stone is long-lasting, low-maintenance, and won't wash away — ideal for borders, drainage areas, and high-traffic spots. Mulch breaks down and needs topping up. We can use stone alone or combine with plantings. Stone holds up to Ontario freeze-thaw and reduces weeds. We help you choose the right type and colour and provide a free written quote.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about landscape stone in Hamilton, Burlington and Oakville.

We regularly install Ontario armour stone (250 to 2,000 lb quarried limestone from Wiarton and Owen Sound), 1 to 3 inch river rock in grey, tan, and Carolina multicolour, 3/4 inch clear crushed stone for bed drainage, Wiarton flagstone steppers, and pea gravel in multiple colours. For decorative highlights we also source marble chip and red granite aggregate.
In 2026, decorative river rock and crushed stone beds run about $8 to $14 per square foot installed (including geotextile fabric, edging, and 2-3 inch stone depth) in Hamilton, Burlington, and Oakville. Armour stone walls run $85 to $140 per linear foot depending on height and stone size. A typical 150 sq ft river-rock front bed lands around $1,500 to $2,000.
Yes, always. We install 4 oz non-woven geotextile fabric (brand names include Mirafi 140N or SRW-Geotex) under every decorative stone bed in Hamilton and Halton. Without fabric, the stone migrates into clay subgrade within 2-3 years and weeds penetrate from below. Proper fabric keeps the bed clean and the stone visible for 15+ years.
Armour stone walls under 1 metre (3 ft 3 in) on clay subgrade require a 12-inch compacted 3/4-clear crushed stone base wrapped in geotextile, with each stone buried 4 to 6 inches deep for stability. Walls over 1 metre need an engineered drawing and a municipal permit in Hamilton, Burlington, and Oakville. We use a Cat 299 skid-steer or Kubota excavator to place stones precisely.
Stone wins for longevity (lasts 15+ years vs mulch needing replacement every 2-3 years), drainage near foundations, and high-traffic areas. Mulch wins for soil health because it breaks down and feeds plants. We often use stone for perimeter beds near walkways and driveways, and shredded bark mulch around trees and perennial beds.
Yes. Single-course armour stone (typically 1 to 2 ft high) is excellent for garden-bed retention on Ancaster or Dundas sloped lots. For anything over 1 metre we specify a dimensional retaining system (Allan Block, Techo-Bloc, or Unilock) with geogrid reinforcement and a permit.
